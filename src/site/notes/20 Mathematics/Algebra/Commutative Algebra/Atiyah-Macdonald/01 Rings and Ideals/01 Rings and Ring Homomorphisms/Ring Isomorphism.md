# Ring Isomorphism

A **ring isomorphism** is a bijective [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/01 Rings and Ring Homomorphisms/Ring Homomorphism\|Ring Homomorphism]]. If $f:A\to B$ is bijective, then its inverse preserves the ring operations, so $A$ and $B$ carry the same ring structure.

We write

$$
A\cong B.
$$

An isomorphism preserves every property stated solely in terms of addition, multiplication, $0$, and $1$: being a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/03 Zero Divisors Nilpotents and Units/Unit\|Unit]], a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/03 Zero Divisors Nilpotents and Units/Zero Divisor\|Zero Divisor]], or a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/03 Zero Divisors Nilpotents and Units/Nilpotent Element\|Nilpotent Element]], for example. It also gives an inclusion-preserving bijection between the ideals of the two rings.

## Central example

For a homomorphism $f:A\to B$, the [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/02 Ideals and Quotient Rings/First Isomorphism Theorem for Rings\|First Isomorphism Theorem for Rings]] gives

$$
A/\ker f\cong \operatorname{im}f.
$$

Thus a quotient is not merely similar to the image: it is the image up to a canonical change of names.

## Nonexample

$\mathbb Z$ and $2\mathbb Z$ are isomorphic as additive groups, but $2\mathbb Z$ is not even a unital subring of $\mathbb Z$ under the convention used here. An additive-group isomorphism need not be a ring isomorphism.
