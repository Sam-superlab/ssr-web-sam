# Principal Ideal

For $x$ in a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/01 Rings and Ring Homomorphisms/Ring\|Ring]] $A$, the **principal ideal generated by $x$** is

$$
(x)=Ax=\{ax:a\in A\}.
$$

It is the smallest [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/02 Ideals and Quotient Rings/Ideal\|Ideal]] containing $x$: every ideal that contains $x$ must contain every multiple $ax$.

More generally, elements $x_1,\ldots,x_n$ generate

$$
(x_1,\ldots,x_n)
=
\left\{\sum_{i=1}^n a_ix_i:a_i\in A\right\}.
$$

## Examples

- In $\mathbb Z$, $(n)=n\mathbb Z$, and every ideal is principal.
- In $k[x,y]$, $(x,y)$ is not principal.
- $(0)$ is the [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/03 Zero Divisors Nilpotents and Units/Zero Ideal\|Zero Ideal]], while $(1)=A$ is the unit ideal.

## Element tests

$$
x\text{ is a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/03 Zero Divisors Nilpotents and Units/Unit\|Unit]]}\iff(x)=A.
$$

Also,

$$
(x)\subseteq(y)\iff x=ay\text{ for some }a\in A.
$$

In an [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/03 Zero Divisors Nilpotents and Units/Integral Domain\|Integral Domain]], a ring in which every ideal is principal is a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Atiyah-Macdonald/01 Rings and Ideals/04 Prime and Maximal Ideals/Principal Ideal Domain\|Principal Ideal Domain]].
