# Module-Finite Algebra

An $A$-algebra $B$ is **module-finite**, or **finite over $A$**, if $B$ is a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Modules/Finite Generation and Local Methods/Finitely Generated Module\|Finitely Generated Module]] over $A$. The structure homomorphism $A\to B$ is then called a finite ring map.

## Examples

- $A/I$ is finite over $A$, generated as a module by $1+I$.
- $A[x]/(p(x))$ is finite over $A$ when $p$ is monic of degree $d$, generated by $1,x,\ldots,x^{d-1}$.

A module-finite algebra is automatically an [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Algebras over Rings/Core/Algebra of Finite Type\|Algebra of Finite Type]], because module generators also generate the algebra. The converse fails: $A[x]$ is generated by one element as an algebra but, for $A\neq0$, requires infinitely many module generators $1,x,x^2,\ldots$.

Do not confuse “finite” with having finitely many elements. Here the word refers to finite generation as a module.
