# Extension and Contraction

Let $f:A\to B$ be a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Rings and Morphisms/Foundations and Morphisms/Ring Homomorphism\|Ring Homomorphism]].

For an [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Ideals and Quotients/Operations/Ideal\|Ideal]] $\mathfrak a\subseteq A$, its image $f(\mathfrak a)$ need not be an ideal of $B$. Its [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Ideals and Quotients/Quotients and Correspondence/Extension of an Ideal\|extension]] is therefore defined as the ideal generated by that image:

$$
\mathfrak a^e=Bf(\mathfrak a)
=
\left\{\sum_i b_i f(a_i):b_i\in B,\ a_i\in\mathfrak a\right\}.
$$

For an ideal $\mathfrak b\subseteq B$, its [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Ideals and Quotients/Quotients and Correspondence/Contraction of an Ideal\|contraction]] is the inverse image

$$
\mathfrak b^c=f^{-1}(\mathfrak b).
$$

Contraction behaves better with primality: if $\mathfrak q$ is [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Ideals and Quotients/Prime Maximal and Radical Ideals/Prime Ideal\|prime]] in $B$, then $\mathfrak q^c$ is prime in $A$. Extension of a prime ideal need not be prime. Under $\mathbb Z\hookrightarrow\mathbb Q$, every nonzero ideal of $\mathbb Z$ extends to the unit ideal of $\mathbb Q$.

## Adjunction

Extension and contraction satisfy

$$
\mathfrak a^e\subseteq\mathfrak b
\iff
\mathfrak a\subseteq\mathfrak b^c.
$$

In particular,

$$
\mathfrak a\subseteq\mathfrak a^{ec},
\qquad
\mathfrak b^{ce}\subseteq\mathfrak b.
$$

The [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Ideals and Quotients/Quotients and Correspondence/Extension-Contraction Correspondence\|Extension-Contraction Correspondence]] describes the ideals fixed by these closure operations; Proposition 1.17 gives

$$
\mathfrak a^e=\mathfrak a^{ece},
\qquad
\mathfrak b^c=\mathfrak b^{cec}.
$$

## Arithmetic example

For $\mathbb Z\subseteq\mathbb Z[i]$, the behavior of $(p)^e$ depends on $p$:

- $(2)^e=(1+i)^2$ up to a unit;
- if $p\equiv1\pmod4$, it splits into two distinct prime factors;
- if $p\equiv3\pmod4$, it remains prime.

This illustrates that extension along an inclusion can encode deep arithmetic.

## Operations

Extension preserves sums and products. Contraction preserves intersections. The reverse directions generally give only inclusions, as summarized in Exercise 1.18.
