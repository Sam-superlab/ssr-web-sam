# 03.00 Rings and Modules of Fractions

## Why localization?

The field of fractions of an integral domain inverts every nonzero element. [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Localization and Fractions/Core/Localization of a Ring\|Localization]] keeps the same idea but allows an arbitrary [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Rings and Morphisms/Foundations and Morphisms/Ring\|Ring]] and a chosen [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Localization and Fractions/Core/Multiplicative Set\|Multiplicative Set]] $S$. This flexibility lets us invert one element, work near a prime, or remove torsion killed by denominators.

## Constructing $S^{-1}A$

Fractions are pairs $(a,s)\in A\times S$. Define

$$
(a,s)\sim(b,t) \quad\Longleftrightarrow\quad u(at-bs)=0
\text{ for some }u\in S.
$$

The extra factor $u$ replaces cancellation, which may fail when $A$ has zero-divisors. Write the class of $(a,s)$ as $a/s$ and use the familiar fraction rules. The canonical homomorphism sends $a$ to $a/1$; it need not be injective, since $a/1=0$ exactly when some $s\in S$ annihilates $a$.

### Universal property

If $g:A\to B$ sends each $s\in S$ to a unit, there is a unique homomorphism

$$
S^{-1}A\longrightarrow B,\qquad a/s\longmapsto g(a)g(s)^{-1}
$$

through which $g$ factors. This is the safest way to define maps out of a localization.

## Standard cases

- $S=A\setminus\mathfrak p$: $S^{-1}A=A_{\mathfrak p}$ is a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Local Algebra/Core/Local Ring\|Local Ring]].
- $S=\{1,f,f^2,\ldots\}$: write $A_f$.
- $S=A\setminus\{0\}$ in a domain: obtain its [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Localization and Fractions/Fraction Rings/Field of Fractions\|Field of Fractions]].
- If $0\in S$, then $S^{-1}A$ is the zero ring.

## Modules of fractions

For an $A$-module $M$, fractions $m/s$ form the [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Localization and Fractions/Modules/Localization of a Module\|Localization of a Module]] $S^{-1}M$. A map $M\to N$ localizes by $m/s\mapsto f(m)/s$. The key result is that [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Localization and Fractions/Modules/Exactness of Localization\|Exactness of Localization]]: kernels and images survive localization in the expected way.

Consequences include

$$
S^{-1}(M/N)\cong S^{-1}M/S^{-1}N,
$$

compatibility with finite sums and intersections, and

$$
S^{-1}M\cong S^{-1}A\otimes_A M.
$$

Thus $S^{-1}A$ is a [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Homological Algebra/Flatness and Tor/Flat Module\|Flat Module]] over $A$, and localization commutes with [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Homological Algebra/Tensor Products/Tensor Product\|tensor products]].

## Proof idea for exactness

If $g(m)/s=0$ after localization, some $t\in S$ satisfies $tg(m)=0$. Exactness before localization gives $tm=f(m')$ for some $m'$. Then

$$
m/s=f(m')/(st),
$$

so the localized kernel lies in the localized image.
