# 04.00 Primary Decomposition

## Primary ideals

A proper [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Ideals and Quotients/Operations/Ideal\|Ideal]] $\mathfrak q$ is [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Primary Decomposition and Associated Primes/Primary Ideals and Decompositions/Primary Ideal\|primary]] if

$$
xy\in\mathfrak q, x\notin\mathfrak q
\quad\Longrightarrow\quad y^n\in\mathfrak q
\text{ for some }n>0.
$$

Equivalently, every zero-divisor of $A/\mathfrak q$ is nilpotent. Its radical $\sqrt{\mathfrak q}$ is prime; if this radical is $\mathfrak p$, then $\mathfrak q$ is $\mathfrak p$-primary.

Every prime ideal is primary. Powers of maximal ideals are primary, but a power of a nonmaximal prime need not be. Likewise, a primary ideal need not equal a prime power: in $k[x,y]$, $(x,y^2)$ is $(x,y)$-primary but lies strictly between $(x,y)^2$ and $(x,y)$.

## Decompositions

A [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Primary Decomposition and Associated Primes/Primary Ideals and Decompositions/Primary Decomposition\|Primary Decomposition]] is a finite expression

$$
\mathfrak a=\bigcap_{i=1}^n\mathfrak q_i
$$

with each $\mathfrak q_i$ primary. It is [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Primary Decomposition and Associated Primes/Primary Ideals and Decompositions/Minimal Primary Decomposition\|minimal]] when the radicals $\mathfrak p_i=\sqrt{\mathfrak q_i}$ are distinct and no component can be omitted. Components with the same radical may first be combined because a finite intersection of $\mathfrak p$-primary ideals is again $\mathfrak p$-primary.

## First uniqueness theorem

The primes $\mathfrak p_i$ are intrinsic. They are exactly the prime ideals appearing among

$$
\sqrt{(\mathfrak a:x)},\qquad x\in A.
$$

These are the [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Primary Decomposition and Associated Primes/Associated Primes and Components/Associated Prime\|associated primes]] of $\mathfrak a$. Minimal elements among them are [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Primary Decomposition and Associated Primes/Associated Primes and Components/Minimal Associated Prime\|isolated primes]]; the remaining ones are [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Primary Decomposition and Associated Primes/Associated Primes and Components/Embedded Prime\|embedded primes]]. Every prime containing $\mathfrak a$ contains an isolated associated prime.

For a decomposable zero ideal, the set of zero-divisors is the union of the associated primes, while the nilradical is the intersection of the isolated primes.

## Localization

A $\mathfrak p$-primary ideal becomes the unit ideal if the multiplicative set meets $\mathfrak p$. Otherwise it remains primary after [[20 Mathematics/Algebra/Commutative Algebra/Concepts/Localization and Fractions/Core/Localization of a Ring\|localization]], and contracts back to itself. Consequently, localizing a decomposition deletes exactly the components whose radicals meet the multiplicative set.

## Second uniqueness theorem

The embedded primary components need not be unique. However, the intersection of the components belonging to any inclusion-downward-closed set of associated primes is independent of the chosen minimal decomposition. In particular, every isolated primary component is unique.
